03 / Personal Digital Security

You are your own IT department.

Most people don't realise their digital life is basically an unlocked house in a bad neighbourhood.

🔐

📲

2FA / MFA

"The extra step that blocks 99.9% of automated attacks." — Microsoft research, 2019.

🔄

Software Updates

Every update patches a vulnerability. Delaying = leaving a known door unlocked.

🎣

"They weren't even hacked. Their vendor was."

08 / Industrial Challenges

The Big 5 Problems India Faces

🧑‍💻 1. The Talent Gap

CRITICAL

3 million unfilled cybersecurity jobs globally. 1 million in India alone. Universities produce engineers. Companies need security engineers. You are literally in the right room at the right time.

💻 2. Legacy Systems

HIGH

Some Indian banks and government agencies still run Windows XP. Infrastructure built 20-30 years ago wasn't designed for modern threats. Upgrading is expensive, risky, and politically slow. Hackers? They love legacy systems.

🧠 3. Human Error

HIGH

78% of successful attacks exploit humans — phishing clicks, weak passwords, misconfigured access. You can have a ₹100 crore security stack. One employee clicks a link. Game over.

🔗 4. Third-Party Risk

MEDIUM

Every vendor, supplier, and contractor is an attack surface. Air India wasn't breached — their vendor was. The average large enterprise has 3,800+ third-party connections.

📋 5. Compliance Theatre

MEDIUM

Many companies do security "for the audit". ISO 27001, SOC 2, PCI DSS — frameworks, not guarantees. Checking boxes ≠ being secure. The attacker doesn't read your compliance report.

💡 Thought-Provoking Insight

"The biggest vulnerability in any system isn't the code. It's the culture — the processes, the people, and the assumption that 'we're probably fine.' Probably fine is how AIIMS ended up on pen and paper for 15 days."

11 / The Original Hack

Kevin Mitnick never used a zero-day.

FBI's most wanted hacker. Weapon of choice: the telephone.

📖 The Story

1990s. Mitnick hacked DEC, Sun Microsystems, Nokia, Motorola — not through exploits, but through conversation.

"Hi, this is Gary from IT support. Our systems are down. I need your login to restore your access."

People said yes. Every time. He wrote "The Art of Deception" — still the bible of social engineering. Today he runs a cybersecurity firm worth millions.

Arrested in 1995. Reformed. Hired by the same companies he hacked.

📧

Phishing

Fake emails. 95% of breaches start here. "Your SBI account is suspended. Click to verify."

📞

Vishing

Voice phishing. "Hi, I'm calling from your bank's fraud department..." Massive in India right now.

📱

Smishing

SMS phishing. "Your Aadhaar is blocked. Click OTP link." ₹1,000+ crore lost in India in 2023.

🚪

Tailgating

Physical. Holding a door open for someone in a suit. Walking into a data center. No hack needed.

💡 Thought-Provoking Insight

"The most sophisticated firewall in the world can't stop someone holding a door open for a stranger in a suit. Social engineering works because it targets trust — and trust is something no software can fully patch."

12 / Beyond the Tech

Technology is 20% of security.
Culture is the other 80%.

Companies that survive breaches vs. those that collapse — the difference is almost never the tools.

🎬 Netflix: "Freedom & Responsibility"

Engineers get full production access. No approval chains. They trust people — but they monitor everything. Incidents are treated as learning opportunities, not firings. Result: fastest incident response in the industry.

🔍 Google: "Project Zero + Bug Bounties"

Google pays researchers to find bugs in their own products. They open-sourced the results. Security became a company-wide obsession, not an IT department problem.

📊 The Pacvue Lens

At Pacvue, data drives every decision — performance, growth, ROI. Apply that same mindset to security: "Security isn't a cost centre. It's a performance metric. If you can't measure it, you can't manage it."

Building security culture — 4 pillars:

1. Psychological Safety

People must feel safe to report mistakes. Blame culture = hidden incidents = massive breaches.

2. Security Champions

Every engineering team has one person whose job includes security. Not just the security team's job.

3. Blameless Post-Mortems

After an incident: ask "what failed?" not "who failed?" Fix the system, not the person.

4. Regular, Real Training

Not annual compliance videos. Monthly phishing drills. Red team exercises. Actual practice.

💡 Thought-Provoking Insight

"The most secure companies in the world aren't necessarily the ones with the biggest security budgets. They're the ones where every employee, from the CEO to the intern, thinks like a security person. That's culture. Culture is your hardest-to-hack asset."
